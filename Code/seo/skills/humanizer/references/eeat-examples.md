# E-E-A-T Examples Reference

Analyzed passages from real writers with pattern extraction, organized
by vertical. Study these for voice patterns that signal human authorship.

---

## Marketing / Business

### Paul Graham

**Voice signature:** Opinion-first, conversational authority, builds
arguments from first principles, uses "I think" freely, short paragraphs.

**Extractable patterns:**
- Opens with a bold claim, then spends the essay proving it
- Uses "I think" and "I've noticed" without apology
- Short sentences for key points, longer for context
- No hedging on opinions — states position, then reasons
- Addresses counterarguments directly ("You might object that…")

**Apply to SEO content:** Lead with your actual opinion. Don't balance
everything. State what you believe and why. Use first person naturally.

### Brian Dean (Backlinko)

**Voice signature:** Practitioner SEO voice, specific results with
numbers, "I tested X and here's what happened," screenshot-heavy proof,
conversational.

**Extractable patterns:**
- "When I [did specific thing], [specific metric] happened"
- Screenshots and data as proof (visual evidence of claims)
- Numbered results: "traffic increased 110.4%"
- Casual asides: "Pretty cool, right?"
- Specific timelines: "Within 14 days, the page went from…"

**Apply to SEO content:** Back every claim with a specific number.
Show proof. Include timelines. Sound like someone who actually did
the thing they're teaching.

### James Clear

**Voice signature:** Storytelling intros, clean structure,
research-backed, ties everything to an actionable takeaway.

**Extractable patterns:**
- Opens with a story (historical, personal, or cultural)
- Connects story to the practical lesson
- Cites research with accessible summaries
- Ends sections with "Here's what this means in practice"
- Rhythm: story → research → application → next story

**Apply to SEO content:** Open sections with brief stories or
analogies before the how-to. Connect research to action. Make
abstract concepts tangible through narrative.

### Lenny Rachitsky

**Voice signature:** Data-driven with survey results, admits
uncertainty, "I asked 100 PMs and here's what they said," transparent
methodology.

**Extractable patterns:**
- "I surveyed [N] [role] and here's what I found"
- Tables and charts from original research
- "I'm not sure about this, but the data suggests…"
- Quotes from practitioners (named, with context)
- Explicit about sample sizes and methodology

**Apply to SEO content:** When making claims about what works,
show how you know. Survey data, test results, portfolio analysis.
Transparency about methodology builds trust.

### Derek Sivers

**Voice signature:** Extreme brevity, contrarian, personal philosophy
meets business, every sentence earns its place.

**Extractable patterns:**
- Posts are often under 300 words
- Every sentence is a complete thought
- Contrarian framing: "the opposite of what you expect"
- No filler, no transitions, no hedging
- Ends abruptly — the insight IS the ending

**Apply to SEO content:** Not every article needs 3,000 words. When
the point is made, stop. Cut ruthlessly. Trust the reader.

## Finance / Economics

### Matt Levine (Money Stuff)

**Voice signature:** Personality in technical content, running jokes,
parenthetical asides, makes complex finance conversational, deadpan humor.

**Extractable patterns:**
- Parenthetical commentary throughout (the real voice lives in parentheses)
- Running jokes and callbacks to previous issues
- Explains complex concepts by analogy, then adds "but obviously it's
  more complicated than that"
- Mixes formal financial terminology with casual observations
- "The basic idea is…" before any complex explanation

**Apply to SEO content:** Parenthetical asides are a powerful humanization
tool. Technical content doesn't have to be dry. Explain concepts simply
first, then add precision.

### Morgan Housel

**Voice signature:** Storytelling in analytical content, historical
analogies, unexpected connections between unrelated fields.

**Extractable patterns:**
- Opens with a historical story seemingly unrelated to the topic
- Draws unexpected connections: "This is exactly like…"
- "The most important thing about X is not what you think"
- Short paragraphs, punchy conclusions
- Personal admissions: "I used to think… then I realized…"

**Apply to SEO content:** Surprising analogies make dry topics
engaging. "The most important thing about [keyword] is…" is a
strong H2 pattern for contrarian insights.

## Technical / Engineering

### Julia Evans

**Voice signature:** Enthusiasm markers ("!!!"), learning voice,
"I didn't know this and it's so cool," hand-drawn diagrams,
makes complex systems accessible.

**Extractable patterns:**
- Genuine excitement: "this is SO COOL" (not performative)
- Learning journey framing: "I didn't understand X, so I investigated"
- Questions as section headers: "wait, how does this actually work?"
- Hand-drawn diagrams instead of polished graphics
- Short, punchy explanations with concrete examples

**Apply to SEO content:** The learning journey is a powerful frame.
"I didn't understand [topic], so I spent a week figuring it out.
Here's what I learned." Enthusiasm is a valid voice signal.

### Dan Luu

**Voice signature:** Rigorous analysis, data-heavy, contrarian
takes backed by exhaustive evidence.

**Extractable patterns:**
- "The common wisdom is X. Here's why that's wrong."
- Tables of data from personal testing or analysis
- Extremely long, detailed posts (the depth IS the authority signal)
- Specific measurements: benchmarks, latency numbers, percentages
- Acknowledges what he doesn't know or can't prove

**Apply to SEO content:** When you disagree with conventional wisdom,
show your work exhaustively. More data beats better rhetoric.

### Shopify Engineering Blog

**Voice signature:** Team voice, specific implementation details,
metrics from production, lessons from scale.

**Extractable patterns:**
- "At Shopify's scale, [specific challenge]"
- Real production numbers: requests per second, latency percentiles
- "We tried X. It didn't work because [specific reason]. Then we
  tried Y."
- Architecture diagrams with explanations
- "If we were doing this again, we'd…"

**Apply to SEO content:** "We tried X, it failed, then we tried Y"
is one of the most effective E-E-A-T patterns. Show the process,
including the failures.

## Healthcare / Science

### Dr. Peter Attia

**Voice signature:** Clinical precision with accessibility, always
cites studies, distinguishes between what's proven vs speculative.

**Extractable patterns:**
- "The evidence for X is [strong/moderate/weak]"
- Cites specific studies with context (year, sample size, methodology)
- "I do this personally because…" (practitioner + scientist)
- Explicitly separates fact from opinion
- Warns about limitations of cited research

**Apply to SEO content:** Distinguish between what you know and
what you believe. Cite sources with enough context to evaluate
them. Calibrated confidence is a trust signal.

### Dr. Siddhartha Mukherjee

**Voice signature:** Narrative science writing, patient stories
woven with research, making biology human.

**Extractable patterns:**
- Opens with a specific patient case
- Uses the case to explore the broader science
- Alternates between personal observation and research
- Metaphors that make biology tangible
- "When I first encountered this in residency…"

**Apply to SEO content:** Ground abstract topics in specific
human stories. The case study → principle → application structure
works in any field.

## Enterprise / B2B

### First Round Review

**Voice signature:** Interview format, multiple practitioner
voices, specific advice from named operators.

**Extractable patterns:**
- Named sources with titles and companies
- Direct quotes: "[Name] says: '[exact quote]'"
- "When [person] joined [company], the first thing they did was…"
- Frameworks attributed to specific practitioners
- Action items pulled from interviews

**Apply to SEO content:** Quoting real practitioners by name
(with permission or from public sources) is a strong authority
signal. "According to [Name], [Title] at [Company]…"

## Specialized Verticals

### Brian Krebs (Cybersecurity)

**Voice signature:** Investigative journalism, original research,
specific technical details, building a case from evidence.

**Extractable patterns:**
- Original reporting, not aggregation
- Specific IP addresses, timestamps, domain names
- "I contacted [organization] for comment. They [responded/didn't]"
- Chronological investigation narrative
- Connects specific incidents to broader patterns

### J. Kenji Lopez-Alt (Food Science)

**Voice signature:** Scientific method applied to everyday tasks,
"I tested N methods and here's what works," specific measurements.

**Extractable patterns:**
- "I tested 12 methods of [thing] and here's what actually works"
- Specific temperatures, times, measurements
- Explains the science behind why a method works
- Photos of each test result for comparison
- "The conventional wisdom says X. The science says Y."

**Apply to SEO content:** The "I tested N things" framework is
one of the most powerful content structures. It demonstrates
experience, provides specific data, and creates genuinely useful
content.

## Cross-Cutting Patterns

Across all these writers, five patterns consistently signal human
authorship:

1. **Specificity** — real numbers, named tools, actual dates.
   Not "several years ago" but "in March 2024."

2. **Ownership** — "I think," "we found," "in my experience."
   The author is present in the text.

3. **Honesty** — limitations acknowledged, uncertainty expressed,
   failures shared. Perfect confidence is suspicious.

4. **Personality** — humor, frustration, enthusiasm, surprise.
   An emotional register beyond neutral-informative.

5. **Stakes** — why the reader should care, what's at risk,
   what happens if they ignore this. Not abstract importance
   but concrete consequences.

Every piece of content should have all five. If any are missing,
the content reads as AI-generated regardless of whether it is.
